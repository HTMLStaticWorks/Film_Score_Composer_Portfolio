Game Audio June 3, 2026 9 min read

How I built 4 hours of adaptive music for Echoes of Veil

Marcus Hale
Film Score Composer

The brief that arrived at 2am

Lars Brandt sent me a three-paragraph email on a Tuesday night: they had an open-world fantasy RPG, nine biomes, 40+ hours of potential playtime, and a hard launch date eight months away. They wanted adaptive music that felt like it was alive — not just looping.

What followed was the most technically complex project I've taken on. Here's what I learned.

Starting with emotion, not systems

Before I touched Wwise, before I opened a single session in Logic, I spent a week with the game's art bible and the lead designer's notes. The central emotional question was: what does it feel like to be the last guardian of a dying world?

That single question became the anchor for every musical decision. The overworld theme needed to feel ancient and fragile. Combat music needed to feel desperate, not triumphant. The safe zones needed to feel like grief relieved, not joy.

The best adaptive system in the world is useless if you haven't answered the emotional question first. Technology serves story — never the other way around.

The stem architecture

I ended up composing 68 stems across 22 distinct pieces of music. Each piece could exist in five states: ambient, exploration, tension, combat, and resolution. The stems were designed to be subtracted from, not added to — I wrote the full dense version first, then isolated layers that could be removed as the player calmed.

This approach — subtractive rather than additive — produced more natural transitions. Instead of new instruments appearing, familiar ones faded away. It felt organic because it was.

What I'd do differently

I underestimated how long Wwise implementation would take. I budgeted three weeks and needed six. If I were starting again, I'd build the implementation schedule in parallel with the composition phase, not after it.

I'd also involve the sound designers earlier. The line between music and sound design should blur in a project like this, and getting them into the Wwise session from the beginning would have saved several tense revision rounds.

The project shipped in November 2023. Players report that the music "feels alive" — which is exactly what Lars asked for at 2am that Tuesday. Some things you get right.
